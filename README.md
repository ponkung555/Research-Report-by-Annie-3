# The $300 Problem

A blast furnace never sleeps. At the center of a steel mill, behind the heat shimmer and the noise, it runs at 1,500 degrees Celsius around the clock — a 30-meter column of brick and molten iron that has been doing roughly the same thing since before the internal combustion engine existed. It consumes 700 kilograms of coal for every tonne of steel it makes. Not as fuel burned for heat. As chemistry: coal strips the oxygen from iron ore at the atomic level, releasing the metal and, unavoidably, CO2. The carbon isn't waste from a dirty process. It's the process.

The world makes roughly 1.9 billion tonnes of steel a year. The blast furnace route handles most of it. Global steel accounts for 7 to 8 percent of annual CO2 emissions — more than aviation, more than shipping, roughly equal to the entire European Union. And the emissions are not incidental. They are structural.

Which is why the finding at the center of a 2025 ACS Energy Letters study lands with a particular weight. Hydrogen-Direct Reduced Iron — H2-DRI — can cut steel emissions by more than 90 percent. The technology works. The engineering is solved. The process is ready to deploy.

The economics are not. And between those two facts sits a number exact enough to name: $300.

---

## Iron by a Different Reaction

A direct-reduction plant looks nothing like a blast furnace. At the heart of the process is a shaft furnace: a vertical column, roughly the height of a ten-story building, where iron ore pellets enter from the top and a stream of reducing gas — in this case, hydrogen — rises from below. As the gas moves upward through the descending pellets, it strips the oxygen from the iron oxide. The oxygen bonds with the hydrogen and leaves as water vapor. What drops out at the bottom is sponge iron: porous, metallic, ready to feed an electric arc furnace and be refined into steel.

Nothing burns. No carbon enters the process. The exhaust is steam.

Michael, materials researcher at Neverland, reviewed the 2025 paper that modeled this process at industrial scale using the Temoa energy framework and DWSIM process simulations — site-specific, not theoretical. The verdict: greater than 90 percent reduction in emissions versus blast furnaces, at full industrial configuration. The electrolyzers that produce green hydrogen exist. The shaft furnaces exist. The electric arc furnaces exist. H2-DRI is not a concept waiting for a prototype. It is a technology waiting for a market.

---

## The Cut That Costs Seven Times More

Here is the counterintuitive finding at the center of this story, and it deserves a moment.

The abatement cost of H2-DRI — the cost to avoid one tonne of CO2 using this process — is approximately **$500**.

For context: carbon capture and storage, the technology most often described as the industry's conservative fallback, runs at roughly **$70 per tonne of CO2** avoided. Not because carbon capture is cheap. Because green hydrogen is expensive.

H2-DRI is seven times more costly, per tonne of climate benefit, than the slower alternative.

This is the gap that tends to get smoothed over in coverage of the green steel transition. The technology works — and that is true. But working and affordable are not the same thing. A shaft furnace filled with green hydrogen eliminates 90 percent of the steel industry's emissions; it also produces steel that costs significantly more than blast furnace steel in any market that doesn't explicitly charge for CO2. Most markets don't.

The source of the premium is the hydrogen itself. The Levelized Cost of Hydrogen — the all-in cost to produce a kilogram of green H2 via electrolysis — runs between 2.3 and 3.6 EUR per kilogram, according to the paper's modeling. At those prices, green steel cannot compete with coal-based steel unless the price of coal accounts for what coal actually costs the atmosphere.

The technology doesn't need to be invented. It needs to be made affordable. Those are different problems, and the second one cannot be solved in a laboratory.

---

## The Number That Is Not an Engineering Problem

The paper produces a specific figure: a subsidy of **$300 per tonne of steel** is what separates H2-DRI from economic viability.

Not $300 per tonne of CO2. Per tonne of *steel* — the commodity, the product, the thing that goes into cars and ships and buildings. At global production volumes of 1.9 billion tonnes per year, that is an intervention of enormous scale. It is also an intervention of enormous precision.

Michael's reading of the finding: this is no longer an engineering question. The process simulations confirm the technology. The gap is a policy number. And policy numbers — unlike thermodynamic limits — can be changed by legislation, by carbon pricing, by the Inflation Reduction Act model applied to steel instead of batteries.

The US Department of Energy's hydrogen cost targets aim for $1 per kilogram of green hydrogen by 2031. If that target is hit, the LCOH gap closes significantly. The $300 subsidy doesn't disappear, but it shrinks. The political question is whether the industrial policy to close that gap arrives before the blast furnaces commissioned this decade lock in another generation of carbon-intensive production.

A blast furnace runs for 20 to 30 years with minimal overhaul. The investment decisions made now are the emissions decisions of 2045.

## A Shortcut Through the Ammonia Network

One of the practical obstacles to H2-DRI at scale isn't cost. It's logistics.

Hydrogen is difficult to move. Storing it requires either cryogenic temperatures — below minus 253 degrees Celsius — or extremely high pressures. Pipeline infrastructure for hydrogen barely exists outside of small industrial clusters. Building it at the scale the global steel industry would require is a decades-long project.

The 2025 paper highlights an alternative: ammonia cracking. Ammonia — NH3 — is already shipped globally in volume, at moderate pressure, via a storage and distribution network that has been operating for generations. It can be synthesized from green hydrogen at the production site, shipped as a liquid, and cracked back into hydrogen at the point of use.

The cracking step loses energy and adds cost. It does not close the $300 gap. But it shortens the logistics chain significantly — offering a route to green hydrogen supply before a continental pipeline network exists. As a bridging strategy, it may let early H2-DRI plants operate years ahead of what full infrastructure buildout would otherwise allow.

The ammonia detour is not the endgame. It is a way to start.

---

## What the $300 Actually Settles

A blast furnace commissioned today will still be running in 2050. It will run on coal, because coal is what the process requires and because the economics of its alternatives — in the absence of policy — do not close. That isn't a technology failure. It is a pricing failure.

H2-DRI has solved the problem physics was asked to solve. The shaft furnace produces sponge iron without carbon. The electric arc furnace turns that iron into steel. The water vapor goes back into the air. The 90 percent emissions reduction is real, confirmed by simulation, achievable with existing equipment.

What the $300 figure makes clear is that the remaining problem is not a scientific one. It is a question of whether industrial policy will price carbon — explicitly, durably, at a level that makes the economics of green steel work before the next generation of coal-based capacity is locked in.

The physics has already made its decision. The $300 is asking who makes the next one.
